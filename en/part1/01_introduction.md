## 🜍 Letter to the Cartographers of the East

*Annotated transcription of the prologue to the "Letter to the Cartographers of the East," written by the philosopher [[Notable Characters|Lysaur of Premia]]*

> *«There is nothing in Marethar that has not already been discovered. But every discovery is a well-documented misunderstanding.»*
> — Lysaur of Premia, dissident philosopher

**Esteemed scholars, merchants, and seekers of equilibrium,**

if you have come to read these lines, you are likely on the verge of setting foot in the heart of Marethar, or you have at least decided to traverse it with your mind. In either case, know that no book, map, or chronicle can truly prepare you for its truth.

Marethar is our center and our mirror. An inner sea, yes — but also a symbolic place where the desires and obsessions of all the peoples who look upon it are reflected. Its shores are studded with proud city-states, tentacled empires, and deserts that speak the language of ancestors. No shore is neutral. No ship sails for trade alone.

Around this sea stretches an ancient continent, whose names shift with the language in which they are pronounced, and whose borders are drawn in ink or blood. The steppes of [[Aridonians|Aridonia]], the river valleys of [[Naharim]], the mountain fortresses of [[Orethians|Orethia]], the coastal colonies of [[Thalassians|Thalassia]]: each region has carved its own way of living, of praying, of fighting, and above all of remembering.

### On This World

In Marethar, technology has the sound of cannon and the smell of hot iron. Armor is heavy, black powder is unstable, ships glide on water guided by star charts and astrological conviction. But for every rifle, there is an idol in a crypt. For every academy, a sect. For every library, a cult of silence.

Science advances — yes — but it has not yet won. And perhaps it never will.

Knowledge travels aboard caravels and pack horses, but also in the form of songs, tattoos, and scrolls kept by blind scribes. Some peoples entrust everything to their gods; others merely fear them. Still others deny their existence with the same fervor with which they chase their power. In some lands, miracles are performed. In others, miracles are tried as fraud.

> *Marginalia inscribed in a hand on the margin of an Orethian codex:*
> "A man may be wise without being just, and just without understanding the world. But in Marethar, he who is neither of these things is dead."

### On Those Who Inhabit It

The peoples of Marethar are not exotic folk. They are us. Or rather, each of us carries at least one of them within: the [[Lysandrians|Lysandrian]] merchant who swears only by profit; the [[Orethians|Orethian]] soldier who never bows his head; the [[Aridonians|Aridonian]] priestess who reads the desert wind. Each has their own morality, their own code, their own god — and if they have none, they build one.

The differences are not merely external. Languages shift form as bodies do; calendars follow different rhythms; sacred colors do not align. A flower offered as a sign of peace in one culture may be an insult in another. A festival may be sacred in one valley and forbidden two hills away.

This makes every encounter both dangerous and precious.

### On What Can Be Done

In Marethar, one may play at surviving, at dominating, at changing. It is a world of exploration, but also of resistance. Here one may found a caravan or dissolve a kingdom. One may defend a ruin or burn a temple. One may rewrite history — but only if willing to pay the price.

None of this is to say you would be wise to leave, no. But to remind you that here words carry weight, and that the most useful maps are those drawn aloud among friends, in the evening, when wine has dissolved fear and the future is still a wager.

> *Author's final note:*
> "Should anyone gather these writings after I am gone, let them know that I lied only about the most dangerous things. The rest is all true. Or true enough that someone has died for it."
