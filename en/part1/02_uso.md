## 🜚 Why Use Marethar in a Campaign

*A guide for those who craft stories*

> *"There are places where one fights for salvation. Here one fights to remember."*

Marethar is a vast, layered, and open setting. It was designed not to be read as lore to memorize, but to be **played, reinterpreted, filled**.
It offers no predestined heroes, no imposed narrative rails, no cosmic wars to resolve: it offers **tensions, complex cultures, historical events in motion, and a living world where conflict is daily, often internal**.

It is an ideal place to tell stories of choice, responsibility, exploration, and memory.

### A World for Those Who Love Moral Conflict

Marethar offers no easy answers. Every decision, even an apparently practical one, carries **ethical and cultural implications**. The use of force, dialogue with religious powers, negotiation between peoples, the possession of artifacts: nothing is neutral.

This makes it perfect for:

* **political campaigns** centered on diplomacy, treaties, disputes between factions within a nation;
* **moral dramas** where characters must choose whether to betray their beliefs, disobey a deity, or negotiate with a historic enemy to prevent a massacre;
* **military chronicles** in which the army is not merely an armed force, but an instrument of domination or liberation.

> *Example*:
> A group of agents tasked with negotiating peace between the Republic of Premia and the Kingdom of Vallan discovers that the accord requires the sacrifice of an unrecognized minority cult. Accepting the compromise would guarantee the end of a war — but at what price?

### Material and Social Culture at the Heart of Play

Marethar takes social structures seriously. Every culture has an internal organization, a relationship to power, a vision of the sacred, of justice, of memory, and of time.

Religion, for instance, **is not merely a pretext for magic**: it is a system of rules, rites, and responsibilities.
Science is not neutral: it is a contested instrument among philosophers, states, and corporations.
Knowledge is often oral, partial, and tied to castes or guilds that decide what may be disclosed.

> This makes the setting particularly well-suited to:

* **historical and archaeological research campaigns** where the very meaning of "truth" is in dispute;
* stories of **religious conflict and syncretism**, between official cults and minority faiths;
* sessions set in **craft or academic milieus**, such as guilds of naval builders, druidic orders, or alchemical colleges.

### Magic Not as System, But as Tension

In Marethar, magic exists — but it has no "universal ruleset." Every magical tradition has its own origins, limits, languages, and rituals.
Magic may derive from ancestors, from spirits, from gods, or from long chains of experiment.
For some it is sacred; for others, illegal; for others still, a resource to exploit politically.

This creates the perfect ground for:

* **investigative campaigns** on misunderstood or forbidden powers;
* **power games between magical orders and sovereign states**;
* adventures where **magic is a perceived threat, even when it is not real**.

> *Example*:
> A border village is accused of harboring an ancient magical relic capable of diverting river routes. The delegation sent to investigate discovers that the "power" may be nothing but a legend… but suspicion alone is sufficient to trigger an armed dispute between two neighboring states.

### Aesthetic and Sensory Experience

Marethar draws from an **expanded Mediterranean imaginary**, refracted through:

* sixteenth-century technology (cannons, star charts, irrigation, fortifications),
* the memory of Bronze Age civilizations (solar rites, ancestor cults, megalithic architecture),
* the splendor and decay of the early Baroque (palaces, masks, patronage, dark science).

This renders every campaign visually and symbolically **dense, fragrant with history**.

> A campaign can evoke:
>
> * religious processions at dusk,
> * crypts lined with frescoes and candles,
> * harbor markets thick with spices, books, scrolls,
> * oil-lit chambers of philosophers,
> * casemates where weapons are blessed before use.

### No Meta-Plot: Only Emergent Materials

The Marethar document imposes no meta-plot to follow.
There are no ongoing global events, nor "official" heroes to pursue.
Everything is described as a **potential space**, ready to be inhabited by characters, choices, and new conflicts.

> Every region holds a precarious balance. Every culture is crossed by fault lines.
> Every religion has its heresy. Every nation has an enemy, external or internal.

This makes Marethar ideal for:

* emergent campaigns, without predetermined plot;
* sandbox play, OSR, or GMless;
* historical evolutions generated at the table.

### Three Recommended Modes of Use

**🔹 Full Immersion:**
Set your entire campaign in Marethar. Choose a starting region, read the cultures involved, and let their tensions emerge.
Useful for those who want **a deep and coherent setting**.

**🔹 Modular Extension:**
Incorporate Marethar as **a region within a broader world**. Ideal if you use your own universe and wish to introduce a credible area with strong internal tensions.
Perfect for zonal or multiregional campaigns.

**🔹 Source of Inspiration:**
Use Marethar as a **bank of cultures, cults, technologies, maps, creatures, and tensions** to reassemble elsewhere.
Every paragraph is designed to be extracted and transplanted.

### What Kind of Players Appreciate It?

* Those who love worlds with **cultural density**, even at the cost of some added complexity.
* Those who seek stories without heroes and villains, but with many partial truths.
* Those who enjoy **building relationships, networks, social maps**.
* Those who want to **reconstruct the past, interpret the present, and shape the future**.

| Feature | What It Offers |

### How to Start a Campaign in Marethar

*A practical guide for the GM*

Starting to play in Marethar does not require reading the entire setting. The world was designed to **function in concentric circles**: you can begin from one point, deepen later, and leave the rest in shadow until needed.

### Recommended Steps:

**1. Choose a localized starting point**
A city, a region, a frontier. A single paragraph is all you need.

> *Example:* "The Diarchy of Ashania, between sacred forests and rural tensions, offers a perfect scenario for intimate, mysterious campaigns — where magic is whispered and nobles no longer trust their own guardians."

**2. Choose two cultures in tension**
Cultural differences are the lifeblood of play. They may involve religion, language, values. Even if you begin with a homogeneous group, the world around them will have to react.

**3. Choose one open conflict, one buried, one forgotten**

> *Open:* a trade war, a religious dispute, an embargo.
> *Buried:* an internal conspiracy, a nascent rebellion.
> *Forgotten:* an unresolved historical event, a suppressed cult, an unmapped ruin.

**4. Create 3 bonds between the PCs and the world**
Familial, cultural, political. Let them emerge through questions at the start, or let them be discovered in play.

**5. Use only 4 sections of the manual**
For the first sessions, these suffice:

* The dominant culture of the place
* The religions involved
* The condensed chronology
* The geography of the region

### Recommended Tones and Thematic Registers

*For a coherent and multifaceted Marethar*

Marethar can accommodate many narrative tones, but some bring out its nature best:

#### **Recommended Tones**

| Tone | Description | Playable Examples |
| ------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Twilight Epic** | The world is vast, but in decay. One fights for what remains. | Last guardians of a dying cult |
| **Gritty Realism** | Glory is dust, kings bleed. Everything has a price. | Negotiating a treaty between sworn enemies |
| **Ambiguous Mysticism** | It is unclear where faith ends and reality begins. | Interpreting signs, omens, apparitions |
| **Cultural Exploration** | The true unknown is not the map, but the people. | A campaign without combat, but with historical consequences |
| **Renaissance Conspiracy** | Intrigues, double loyalties, masks, and silences. | The arrest of a philosopher changes the fate of a city |

#### **Narrative Aesthetic**

* **Evoke the weight of tradition.** Characters are immersed in stories larger than themselves.
* **Leave room for ambiguity.** Not everything is explained — and often it won't be.
* **Beauty coexists with ruin.** A dome may collapse while prayers are offered beneath it.
* **Words matter.** An edict can change more than a sword.
