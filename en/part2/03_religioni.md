## 🜨 Religions and Beliefs

*In Marethar, faith is what remains when the world breaks*

> *"We pray so that our bones remember what our mouths have forgotten."*
> — Naharim proverb

### The Sect of Shahin

*The Tree That Walks in Bones*

No one knows where Shahin was born. It is not the name of a man, nor of a god in the sense of the great imperial theologies. For the Aridonians, Shahin is **what grows when memory takes root**: a tree planted in the places of spilled blood, that blossoms in hearts that do not forget.

The Sect of Shahin has no temples in the common sense, but **sacred groves** that spread around wells, rough stone altars, or simple perpetual fires. Its priestesses do not write: they **sing genealogies, plant trees, pour water**, and speak with the mothers before the gods.

Its rites are agricultural, but also funerary. They heal, but they also know how to brand. Some of the most terrible curses known in the far south come from a whisper intoned beneath a certain tree, at a certain hour.

Shahin protects, but is slow. It does not intervene to save individuals, but **drowns those who sever bonds of blood**.

> "He who cuts the mother's tree falls beneath the son's shadow."
> — ritual saying incised on a dry leaf, burned during the rite of passage into adulthood

Internal heresies divide the cult between those who consider Shahin a "maternal" entity and those who interpret it as an **impersonal spiritual process**. This has caused fractures not only doctrinal but military. Entire tribes have changed their names and lineages to avoid being associated with versions of the cult deemed "sacrilegious."

### The Faith of Shemshahva

*The serpent remembers longer than time*

High in the Orethian mountains, where the cold preserves the dead and caves carry echoes of ancient names, lives the Faith of Shemshahva. It does not venerate a god in the vertical sense of the word, but **honors an unbroken line of ancestors** whose spirits are still active, judging, hungry for truth.

Shemshahva is depicted as **a brown cobra that sheds its skin once every generation**. Its image is incised on metal, but never prayed to directly. The spirits speak only when they are listened to, and only those who know how to listen can remain sane afterward.

Rites are performed in cavities dug by the dead themselves. Shamans are **archivists of blood**: storytellers, judges, and at times ritual executioners.

But the cult is not only of the mountains: it has spread into the outskirts of mining cities, among scattered clans, and across battlefields. **To wear the symbol of Shemshahva at the neck means that someone, somewhere, is still watching you.**

> "Do not seek vengeance. Speak to the blood: it is already seeking it for you."
> — inscription on a ceremonial dagger, found at Kal-Naruth

Some maintain that Shemshahva and Tudeshkhast are **the same spirit, seen through different eyes**: one as memory, the other as destiny. No one has yet found the courage to write this in an official treatise.

### The Cult of Carthara

*Those who protect the routes protect the names*

Carthara has no face. Or has many. It is the name that appears carved into the wood of holds, on hand-stitched sails, in the dreams of sailors. The Thalassians say that Carthara **is not a deity, but a pact**: a pact between the sea and those who do not betray it.

Every voyage begins with an offering to Carthara: a coin, a fragment of rope, an empty plate left on the water. There is no formal clergy. **Every captain is a priest. Every sailor may call upon Carthara, but only once in a lifetime.**

Its cult often merges with that of Thalaran, but **has a more practical, more severe tone**. While Thalaran speaks of cycles and harmony, Carthara speaks of survival and loyalty. Its prayers are oral contracts. Its miracles: ships that do not sink when they should.

> "He who speaks the name of Carthara twice in a row declares himself ready to die for someone else."
> — unwritten rule among the fishermen of Caladir

The cult has spread beyond the Thalassians: merchants, smugglers, contractors, and even explorers practice it in private. Some carry **inverted astrolabes**, dried-water talismans, or ropes broken in three: signs that, according to legend, **Carthara recognizes at first sight**.

### The Golat Tradition

*The Panther has no need of temples*

Golat is presence, not word. It is seen in the sands that move on their own, in the windless nights, in the yellow eyes that appear and disappear at the edge of the fire.
The Golat Tradition **possesses no dogmas**, no texts, no hierarchies. It is **transmitted through signs, dreams, hummed stories, and gestures among initiates**.

According to believers, Golat is a Yellow Panther: guardian spirit, warrior ancestor, liminal force that watches and protects. It does not judge. But those who betray its trust often die **without witnesses, without visible wounds, and with their mouths full of sand**.

Rituals take place in the open, never in the same place twice. Itinerant shamans, often marginalized by the very Aridonians who venerate Shahin, **keep the panther alive in songs and nocturnal dances**. Some claim that dreams in which Golat appears mark an irreversible passage: one becomes part of its shadow.

> "Golat does not speak. But it always listens."
> — inscribed on a ritual drum of human skin, preserved at Falah-Sen

Rival cults accuse it of sorcery, but those who invoke it **do so for protection, for guidance, or to extinguish the rage of an offended spirit**. No authority has succeeded in eliminating it, nor in controlling it.

### The Gods of Ralertis

*The Sapphire Reaper and the discipline of immortality*

Ralertis is ordered power. It is the name most spoken in imperial courts, in Aurelian temples, in secular tribunals. It is not a god of grace or clemency, but of **form, measure, and final perfection**.

It is said that those who die in the name of Ralertis are not reborn, but **become eternal principle**: a note inscribed in the Cosmos. For the Aurelians, this is desirable.

Its cult is hierarchical, with rites marked by ceremonial clocks, geometric dances, and sacred readings in dead languages. The faithful wear **symbols sewn inside their garments, never visible**, as a sign of inner order.

Ralertis guides judges, architects, philosophers, but also ritual assassins and purifiers. Its temples are **towers empty at the center**, as a reminder that only the void has perfect form.

> "He who leaves shadows in his corner does not deserve the temple."
> — moral code of the Brotherhood of Ralertis (eastern section)

Cyclical heresies maintain that Ralertis is **a demon of inertia, not a god**, and that perfection kills. These movements are suppressed with speed and discretion.

### The Spirits of Thalaran

*Every wave has a voice. Every death a name*

Thalaran is **the sea itself, conscious and fragmented**. The Thalassians do not worship it as a god, but **converse with its parts**: the currents, the winds, the depths, the creatures no one sees anymore.

The rites of Thalaran are collective, sung, navigated. Coastal villages follow the rhythm of the tides, and every generation has a "listener": a figure chosen by the sea, who enters trance during storms.
Many of these never regain clarity, but speak with a salted voice until death.

The spirits of Thalaran **make no promises**. They protect those who honor them, but can overturn a ship simply for having been forgotten for three days. Some describe them as **memories fused with water**: ancestral consciousnesses that reactivate.

> "When you sink, you are not alone. You are with those who remember you."
> — ritual inscription on funerary oars

Thalaran is often confused with Carthara, but while the latter is pact, Thalaran is **reaction and echo**. Older. More unpredictable. And more difficult to deceive.

### The Druidic Orders of Dimilalica

*The cycle that breathes beneath the stone*

Dimilalica is not a deity. It is a concept, a system, a way of perceiving **the hidden symmetry in seasons, animals, minerals**. Its druids seek no power: they seek balance. But in Marethar, every balance is also political tension.

The official doctrine is widespread among rural cultures and some autonomous communities of the interior. Druids live in mobile groups, interpret signs of the wind, read cracks in the earth, and **preserve rituals in oral form, often in encoded verse**.

Many of them refuse writing. The so-called **Apocrypha of Dimilalica** hold that every piece of transcribed knowledge loses its power. Others attempt an "emergency codification," fearing the knowledge will be lost.

> "He who writes a prayer, kills it. But he who does not pass it on, buries it."
> — apocryphal motto handed down among the forests of Velhan

In recent times, some druids have aligned with radical movements, opposing public works, dams, and magical academies. But **no one can say where faith ends and guerrilla begins**.

### Heresies, Syncretisms, Alternative Currents

In Marethar, **every religious truth is contested. Every cult, crossed by divergences**.

* The **Sect of the Victory Bull**, venerated among certain Thalassian clans, is a warlike form of the Creator Adrarad. Its ceremonies involve the ingestion of blood and ritual shouting.
* The **Circles of Double Light**, active among Shahin pilgrims, hold that life and death are not opposites but identical. Some practice planting on human graves.
* The **Fragmentists**, a mystical group born in Fahya, believe that every god is part of a single shattered entity. Accused of anarchic syncretism, many have been expelled from the city-states.
* The **Cult of the Fixed Shadow**, banned in three regions, holds that miracles are the echo of a primordial event already concluded: everything that happens is merely repetition. Its rites are celebrated in darkness, in silence, before mirrors.

#### The Influence and Geography of Faith

*Marethar does not have one sky, but many reflections*

The religions of Marethar do not distribute themselves like political borders, but like **cultural veins**: they cross states, concentrate in cities, dry up in certain rural areas. Some faiths are dominant by law. Others survive by custom. Others still insinuate themselves where the first have failed.

In the Aridonian south, the Sect of Shahin **organizes rural life and land management**: every village has a sacred grove, every market begins with a vegetal blessing. However, at the desert margins and along nomadic routes, the Yellow Panther, Golat, **offers more direct, less codified protection**, better suited to mobile clans and risky commerce.

In Rashia, the cult of Shemshahva **founds the authority of the mountain dynasties**: oaths are sanctified with offerings to ancestors and interpreted by official shamans. But in the courts of the empire, it is Tudeshkhast, god of hierarchies and war, who determines law, often ignoring bonds of blood in favor of consolidated power.

In the imperial center, the religion of Ralertis **is official, carved into codes and civic rites**. It is present in tribunals, in the temples of power, in treaties. Yet in the ports of Linia and in the poor quarters of Marnorum, the cult is perceived as distant: there, syncretic practices spread: semi-clandestine cults of the Ordered Void, or rites devoted to the Fragment, a subversive form of perfection.

On the Thalassian coasts, **Thalaran has no temples, but voice**: its faith is passed down in songs, in fishing shifts, in storms avoided. But it is Carthara, spirit of the naval pact, who is invoked for every commercial contract, even in non-Thalassian ports, where its name is spoken as a gesture of surety between strangers.

Finally, in Fahya, where knowledge and magic intertwine, **faiths become school and censorship**. The cult of Inner Vision, a deviant form of Dimilalica's druidism, survives as an unregistered current, tolerated only so long as it does not speak too loudly. In Premia, meanwhile, **secularism is a fragile pact**: the Senate officially recognizes no deity, but every senator has a private altar. And every law, they say, is only a prayer inscribed in stone rather than papyrus.

### What Is True, and for Whom?

There are no shared answers in Marethar.
Some maintain that deities exist only insofar as they are venerated. Others say that **deities exist, but lie**.
The oldest, simply, **fall silent when an absent god is named**.

> "He who needs to know whether it exists has never truly invoked."
> — Naharim saying, attributed to no one
