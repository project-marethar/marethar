# Spirits of Thalaran

*Every wave has a voice. Every death a name*

Thalaran is **the sea itself, conscious and fragmented**. The Thalassians do not worship it as a god, but **converse with its parts**: the currents, the winds, the depths, the creatures no one sees anymore.

The rites of Thalaran are collective, sung, navigated. Coastal villages follow the rhythm of the tides, and every generation has a "listener": a figure chosen by the sea, who enters trance during storms.
Many of these never regain clarity, but speak with a salted voice until death.

The spirits of Thalaran **make no promises**. They protect those who honor them, but can overturn a ship simply for having been forgotten for three days. Some describe them as **memories fused with water**: ancestral consciousnesses that reactivate.

> "When you sink, you are not alone. You are with those who remember you."
> — ritual inscription on funerary oars

Thalaran is often confused with Carthara, but while the latter is pact, Thalaran is **reaction and echo**. Older. More unpredictable. And more difficult to deceive.

## Extended Profile

**Type:** Folk
**Form:** Animism
**Culture:** Thalassian
**Area:** Thalassia
**Faithful:** 6,621,114

**Description:** The Spirits of Thalaran represent one of the oldest religions of Thalassia. This faith is deeply rooted in the maritime and coastal traditions of the Thalassians, who venerate the spirits of nature and of seafaring ancestors. The Spirits of Thalaran are considered protectors of the sea, the winds, and the creatures of the deep. The faithful believe that every element of nature possesses a spirit, and that ancestors continue to influence the world of the living through these spirits.

**Origins and Religious Practices:** The religion's origins reach back to the first inhabitants of the Thalassian coasts, who sought protection and guidance to face the challenges of the sea. Rituals include offerings of fish and other seafood, ritual chants, and dances celebrating the natural forces. Ceremonies are often conducted on the beach or aboard small vessels at sunset, a moment considered sacred.

**Clergy Structure:** The religion has no rigid clerical structure. Community elders and ships' captains serve as spiritual guides, leading the rituals and transmitting oral traditions to new generations.
