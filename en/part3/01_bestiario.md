## 🜏 Mythic Creatures and Folk Beliefs

*Don't ask yourself if they exist. Ask yourself why everyone speaks of them.*

> *"Every village has a monster. Those that don't simply forgot where it lives."*
> — A rural saying common in the Ashanian countryside

In Marethar, **legendary creatures do not dwell only in forests, deserts, or abysses**. They live in stories. In threats whispered to children. In the journals of explorers who never returned.
Some are tangible, dangerous entities. Others are **"collective dreams"**: they exist because they are spoken of, and they are spoken of because people fear they might exist.
Many scholars of the academies disagree on which ones are real. But in every region, there are those who swear they have seen them.

### From Marethar and Its Depths

#### **Leviathan**

A colossus of the deep. They say it surfaces without warning and sinks entire fleets without ever touching them. Its appearances follow **cycles that only the coastal druids pretend to understand**. Some call it "the wrathful heart of Marethar."

> **Game potential**: it surfaces during a naval negotiation, rendering every human plan useless.
>

#### **Sirens of Marethar**

They sing among the rocks and lure ships. They do not always kill. Some save. Some speak. Every captain knows at least one song said to ward off sirens. None works twice.

> **Game potential**: a survivor tells of a siren who spoke to him… with the voice of his dead mother.
>

### From the Desert and the Winds That Lie

#### **Desert Ifrit**

Walking flames, incarnations of fury and buried secrets. Some tribes venerate them as messengers of Shahin; others hunt them. No one has ever counted the same Ifrit's shape twice.

> **Game potential**: an Ifrit follows the characters, but burns only what they carry — never what they touch.
>

#### **Roc of Marethar**

A giant predatory bird. They say it darkens the sun with a single wingbeat and feeds its chicks on caravan masters. Some insist it is the incarnation of the eastern wind.

> **Game potential**: the PCs find the bones of a caravan… on a plateau with no way up.
>

### From the Mountains and the Places That Want to Stay Buried

#### **Ice Dragon**

It slept. Now it does not. Some say it breathes no fire — it freezes thoughts. It appears only on peaks no one scales anymore. Its shadow falls without making a sound.

> **Game potential**: the spirits of dead climbers serve it as a silent oracle.
>

#### **Stone Golem**

Created to guard the mines, some are still active. They defend passages that not even the dwarves remember. Some speak in ancient verse; others wait for an order that never came.

> **Game potential**: a golem has slept for centuries… and a botched ritual wakes it.
>

### From the Forests and the Rivers That Listen

#### **Rainbow Serpent**

River and divinity at once. Those who see it often change. Those who fear it placate it with song. The Naharim say **it is the breath of Dor itself**.

> **Game potential**: the river changes course to avoid it… leaving a village without water.
>

#### **Phoenix of Linia**

It is reborn, but never as the same creature. Its tears heal, but at a steep price. Some say **it also burns memories**.

> **Game potential**: a city wants to capture it as a national symbol. She… disagrees.
>

### From the Ruins and the Places That Remember Too Much

#### **Ruin Fae**

Beautiful. Lethal. She dwells in the remains of fallen civilizations. **She speaks with those who know what has been lost**. She steals gold from those who seek glory, but gives secrets to those who offer poetry.

> **Game potential**: she asks the PCs to recount the greatest mistake of their own civilization.
>

#### **Golden Griffin**

It soars high above dying empires. It appears only when a power is on the verge of collapse or rebirth. Some seek it out to ask for judgment. No one has ever written down its answer.

> **Game potential**: the griffin circles above the city during a political funeral.
>

### How to Use Them in Play

These creatures are not "monsters." They are **incarnations of memories, fears, dreams, and collective desires**.
Some are events. Others, living metaphors. Introducing them into play **does not mean fighting them, but deciding what they represent**.

> **Narrative use ideas**:

* As signs of historical or moral change (e.g., the golden griffin)
* As ritual or environmental complications (e.g., a marsh hydra blocking access to a sunken temple)
* As opportunities for alliance, transformation, or cultural shift (e.g., the rainbow serpent as spiritual ordeal)
* As deception: the creature exists only in dreams, but influences real decisions
