## 🝑 Activating the Materials

*Things are not useful for what they do. They are useful for how they change whoever touches them.*

> *"Not all objects are useful. Some are inevitable."*

Everything presented in Part III, legendary creatures, contested relics, marginal fragments, and unanswered questions, is not meant to be "inserted" into a story. These are **weights that shift the narrative balance**, elements that **change the shape of the world** the moment they are observed.

### Mythic Creatures

These are not monsters to be fought. They are omens, dream-knots, consequences held in waiting.
Those who encounter them rarely emerge victorious, but almost always emerge changed.

**Possible uses:**

* Off-scene presence (sightings, tracks, shared dreams).
* Ritual entity: a creature appears *because something was said* or *left unsaid*.
* Symbolic interlocutor: it does not speak, but responds to gestures, errors, offerings.
* Moral test: it reacts to truth, to fear, or to faith.

> *Example*: a golden griffin circles the city on the day a new ruler is proclaimed. A good omen? Only if no one pretends to believe it.

### Relics and Legendary Objects

They do not matter for what they do. They matter for what they imply.
Some grant power; others draw consequences. None is ever neutral.

**Possible uses:**

* Narrative MacGuffin: entire factions move only because the object *might* exist.
* Moral object: activatable only by someone who has done something irreversible.
* Ritual lie: everyone knows it is a forgery, but destroying it would break a balance.
* Revealer: the object shows something *not of the world, but of whoever uses it*.

> *Example*: an amulet that protects only those who have never betrayed. The characters receive it... but for whom does it truly work?

### Fragments and Marginalia

They do not exist to explain. They exist to disturb.
They are the narrative equivalent of a whisper at the wrong moment.
One sentence too many. A document someone wished forgotten.

**Possible uses:**

* Session epigraph: opens with a tone that colors every scene that follows.
* Ambiguous clue: contradicts the official account.
* Surviving memory: found in a ruin or a censored library.
* Narrative mirror: speaks indirectly of the character or the group.

> *Example*: a wall graffito: "This is not the city. It is only its memory."

### Open Questions

They do not need to be resolved. They need to be chosen.
Every campaign may answer differently, and the answer changes Marethar.

**Possible uses:**

* Thematic engine: the campaign is built around a question.
* Partial revelation: the answer appears... but in a form no one understands.
* Factional trigger: each group holds a different answer, and wants to impose it.
* Mystic key: the question *is* the ritual. Whoever speaks it aloud changes things.

> *Example*: "What is Shahin?" If the answer is *shared memory*, then whoever lies breaks the cult.
